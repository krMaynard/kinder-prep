# Month 1, Week 2 — CVC Blending: -at, -an, -ap Families / Subitizing 1–5

## Goals
- Reading: Blend printed CVC words in the -at, -an, and -ap word families; connect letter sounds to print while reading 8–10 new words.
- Math: Recognize dot patterns for 1 through 5 instantly without counting; connect all five dot patterns to the correct numerals.

## Day 1 — Meet the -at Family

**Reading (8 min):** Write these words on index cards, one per card: *cat, bat, hat, mat, rat, sat*. Say, "All these words are cousins — they all end in /at/!" Hold up *cat* and model: "/k/... /at/... cat!" Then point to *bat*: "What's the first sound? /b/! And the family ending? /at/! So it says... bat!" Have your child blend *hat, mat, rat* with you pointing to each letter. Say, "Touch the letters, say it slow, then say it fast!"
**Math (5 min):** Introduce dot cards for 4 and 5 (add to the 1–3 cards from last week). Flash 4-dot cards (square pattern) and 5-dot cards (dice pattern). Say, "Your brain already knows 1, 2, 3 — now meet 4 and 5!" Flash each card for one second; child shouts the number. Mix in 1–3 for review. Do 10 rounds total.
**App:** Phonics Blender — tap *c*, *a*, *t* cards, then press Blend. Repeat with bat, hat.
**Reward:** A "Word Family" star — one for each -at word they blend correctly (up to six).

## Day 2 — Meet the -an Family

**Reading (8 min):** Write on cards: *can, man, fan, pan, ran, van*. Say, "Here's a new family — the /an/ family! They all end in /an/." Hold up *can* and point: "/k/... /an/... can! Your turn — what's this one?" (Point to *man*.) Sound out *fan, pan, ran* together, pointing to each letter in sequence. Then mix a few -at cards in and ask your child to sort them: "Is this an -at word or an -an word?"
**Math (5 min):** Play "Five Frame Flash." Draw a five-frame (a row of five boxes) on paper. Fill in 1–5 dots in different configurations and flash it. Ask, "How many filled boxes?" Emphasize: a full five-frame is always 5. Say, "When all the boxes have a dot — that's five, every time!" Do 8 rounds.
**App:** Phonics Blender — blend *can, man, fan* with letter taps and the Blend button.
**Reward:** A crayon drawing of a "sound family house" — they draw a house and write one word from each family on it.

## Day 3 — Meet the -ap Family

**Reading (8 min):** Write on cards: *cap, map, nap, rap, tap, zap*. Say, "A third word family — /ap/! Listen: /k/... /ap/... cap!" Practice blending *map, nap, tap* by sliding a finger under each letter as you say the sound. Then do a quick "family relay" — shuffle all the -at, -an, -ap cards and take turns flipping and reading. Parent reads one, child reads one. Say, "Touch it, say it slow, say it fast!"
**Math (5 min):** "Dot Showdown" — you and your child each flip a dot card (1–5) at the same time and race to shout the number. The fastest correct answer wins a token (use pennies or buttons). Play until someone has five tokens. This adds excitement while reinforcing all five subitizing quantities.
**App:** Lesson Player — loads a three-family word sort with -at, -an, -ap picture cards.
**Reward:** A "Three Families" sticker showing three houses — one for each word family mastered.

## Day 4 — Word Family Mix and Read

**Reading (8 min):** Shuffle all 18 cards from the week (-at, -an, -ap words). Flip them one at a time and have your child blend each word. Time them with a fun "reading speedometer" — tap the table fast if they're speeding through! If they hesitate, say, "What's the first sound? Good — now what's the family?" Aim to get through all 18 cards. Celebrate any they read without pausing as "fast reads."
**Math (5 min):** Play "Build It." Call out a number from 1–5 and your child arranges that many blocks or crackers in the matching dot-card pattern (e.g., 5 in a dice pattern). Then you flash the matching dot card to confirm. This moves from recognition to production, deepening the mental image.
**App:** Phonics Blender — free-choice blend session; let your child pick three words to build.
**Reward:** A "Speed Reader" sticker for every card read in under four seconds.

## Day 5 — Read and Draw

**Reading (8 min):** Say, "You've met three word families this week — let's see if you remember them all!" Call out a word from the week (*hat, van, tap, cat, pan, map*) and have your child blend it aloud, then draw a quick picture of it on paper. This adds meaning and memory. Aim for six words. After each drawing, re-read the word together. Say, "You didn't just say it — you read it and drew it. That's real reading!"
**Math (5 min):** "Subitizing Bingo" with a 5-square card (dot patterns 1–5 in random order). Flash a dot card, and they cover the matching square. Complete a row to win. Play two rounds. Ask, "How did you know that was 4 without counting?" This metacognitive question builds awareness of their own pattern-recognition skill.
**App:** Lesson Player — Friday celebration activity with a reading progress animation.
**Reward:** A "Week 2 All-Star" badge — large sticker or hand-drawn medal on the chart.

## Week 2 Checklist
- [ ] Blends -at words: cat, bat, hat, mat, rat, sat
- [ ] Blends -an words: can, man, fan, pan, ran, van
- [ ] Blends -ap words: cap, map, nap, tap, zap
- [ ] Can sort words into correct word families when cards are mixed
- [ ] Recognizes dot patterns for 4 and 5 without counting
- [ ] Recognizes all dot patterns 1–5 quickly and accurately

## Parent Notes
Now print matters — point to each letter as your child sounds it out. The left-to-right sweep with a finger is building early tracking habits. Don't skip this physical gesture; it becomes automatic later.

When they misread a word, don't say "wrong." Instead, say "Let's check — what's the first sound?" then work through it letter by letter. The process of self-correction is more valuable than getting it right instantly.

For the -ap family, watch for children who skip *rap* and *zap* as "too silly" — lean into silly! Silly words are still phonetically valid and very fun to blend.

For subitizing, if 4 and 5 are still slow, spend extra time on the five-frame. The frame gives a structural anchor ("4 is one short of full") that helps even when pure memory fails.
