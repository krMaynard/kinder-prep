# Month 1, Week 3 — CVC Blending: -ig, -in, -it, -ot, -op Families / Numerals 11–15

## Goals
- Reading: Blend CVC words with short-i and short-o vowels across five new word families; read 15+ new words by end of week.
- Math: Recognize, read, and write numerals 11 through 15; understand that teen numbers are "ten and some more."

## Day 1 — The -ig and -in Families

**Reading (8 min):** Write on cards: *big, dig, fig, jig, pig, wig* and *bin, fin, pin, sin, tin, win*. Introduce short-i: "This vowel says /i/ — like *itch*! Listen: /b/... /i/... /g/... big!" Blend *dig, pig, wig* together. Then introduce -in: "Same vowel, new ending — /in/! /p/... /in/... pin!" Have your child blend *bin, fin, tin*. Alternate between -ig and -in cards to keep them on their toes.
**Math (5 min):** Introduce 11 and 12. Show 11 objects (blocks or beans). Say, "Ten and one more is eleven — 11!" Write the numeral 11 and let your child trace it. Then make 12 objects. "Ten and two more is twelve — 12!" Trace 12. Say, "Teen numbers are sneaky — they always have a ten hiding inside!"
**App:** Phonics Blender — blend *big, pin, dig, win* with letter taps.
**Reward:** A "Short-i Champion" star sticker for each -ig or -in word blended correctly.

## Day 2 — The -it Family

**Reading (8 min):** Write on cards: *bit, fit, hit, kit, lit, pit, sit, wit*. Say, "Here's another short-i family — /it/! Listen: /s/... /it/... sit!" Model the blend, then hand cards to your child one at a time: *bit, fit, hit, kit*. For tricky ones like *wit*, say, "First sound? /w/! Family? /it/! So it says...?" After blending all eight, do a "speed round" — flip and read as fast as possible. Say, "These are all real words — you're reading real English!"
**Math (5 min):** Introduce 13 and 14. Show 13 objects: "Ten and three more is thirteen — 13!" Write the numeral and trace. "Thirteen is a funny word — can you hear the three hiding in thir-TEEN?" Repeat with 14: "Ten and four more is fourteen — 14!" Trace the numeral. Ask, "What's hiding in four-TEEN?" (Four!)
**App:** Lesson Player — loads the -it family word slide with pictures for each word.
**Reward:** An "8-Word Reader" badge — a special sticker for reading all eight -it words.

## Day 3 — The -ot Family

**Reading (8 min):** Write on cards: *cot, dot, got, hot, jot, lot, not, pot*. Say, "New vowel alert — short-o says /o/ like *octopus*! Listen: /h/... /o/... /t/... hot!" Blend *dot, got, pot, not* together. Ask: "Is this /a/ or /o/ in the middle?" (Point to a card.) Hold up a -at card alongside a -ot card and compare: "Cat — /a/. Dot — /o/. Hear the difference?" This vowel discrimination is key.
**Math (5 min):** Introduce 15. Show 15 objects: "Ten and five more is fifteen — 15!" Write and trace the numeral 15. Then play "What number am I?" — hold up 11, 12, 13, 14, or 15 fingers (using two hands, show 10 on one hand and the extra fingers on the other). Child calls out the number. Reinforce: "I always start with a full ten!"
**App:** Phonics Blender — blend *hot, dot, pot, not* — listen for the /o/ sound in each.
**Reward:** A "Vowel Spotter" sticker — one for correctly identifying the vowel in each word (up to eight).

## Day 4 — The -op Family

**Reading (8 min):** Write on cards: *cop, hop, mop, pop, stop, top*. Say, "Last new family this week — /op/! Listen: /h/... /op/... hop!" Note that *stop* has four phonemes (/s/ /t/ /o/ /p/) — a great stretch bonus. Blend all six words. Then do a "grand mix" — shuffle all five families' cards from the week (-ig, -in, -it, -ot, -op) into one pile and flip through 10 random cards for your child to blend. Say, "Your brain knows so many word families now!"
**Math (5 min):** "Teen Number Bingo" — make a 5-square bingo card with the numbers 11–15 written in random squares. Call out numbers or flash written numeral cards, and your child covers the matching square with a coin or block. First to cover all five wins. Play two rounds. Ask, "Can you write the number 13 in the air with your finger?"
**App:** Lesson Player — five-family word sort activity with picture support.
**Reward:** A "Five-Family Star" — a large star they color in themselves on the reward chart.

## Day 5 — Vowel Detective Review

**Reading (8 min):** Mix cards from -at, -an, -ap (Week 2) with this week's -ig, -in, -it, -ot, -op families — about 20 cards total. Hold up each card and ask: "What vowel is hiding in the middle?" Child names the vowel sound (/a/ or /i/ or /o/) and then reads the whole word. Say, "You're a vowel detective — find the hidden sound!" This reinforces the critical skill of vowel identification inside words.
**Math (5 min):** Numeral writing practice: say a number from 11–15 and your child writes it on a small whiteboard or paper. Use a verbal cue for tricky numerals: "Eleven is 1-1, two ones standing together." "Fifteen has a one and a five." Do all five numbers twice. Say, "You know your teen numbers — that's something most kindergartners learn in school!"
**App:** Phonics Blender — free blend session; child picks their five favorite words from the week to build and blend.
**Reward:** A "Week 3 Decoder" badge — celebrate knowing five word families and five teen numbers.

## Week 3 Checklist
- [ ] Blends -ig words: big, dig, pig, wig
- [ ] Blends -in words: bin, pin, fin, win
- [ ] Blends -it words: bit, sit, hit, fit, kit
- [ ] Blends -ot words: hot, dot, pot, not, got
- [ ] Blends -op words: hop, mop, pop, top
- [ ] Recognizes and reads numerals 11–15
- [ ] Can write numerals 11–15 when called out
- [ ] Understands teen numbers as "ten and some more"

## Parent Notes
This week introduces short-o, which is often confused with short-a. If your child reads *dot* as "dat," slow down and exaggerate: "Listen — /o/, like when a doctor says 'open up — ahhhh.'" Keep a "vowel anchor word" posted nearby: A=apple, I=itch, O=octopus.

The -it family has the most words (8), so Day 2 is the heaviest reading day. If your child is tiring, do the first five words, then save the last three for Day 3 warm-up.

For teen numbers, use physical ten-frames made from an egg carton (12 cups = one full row of 10 plus 2 extras). Seeing a physical ten-and-more builds deeper number sense than numerals alone. Writing teen numbers can be tricky — 12 written as "21" is a very common reversal; gently correct by pointing: "In twelve, the 1 comes first."
